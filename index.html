Case Study · AI / QA Systems
Testing LLM Systems: Hybrid QA Framework

Developed a QA evaluation framework for LLM-generated summaries used by 50,000+ users, processing an average of 3.5M+ output tokens per month in a production environment. Mostly used for generating job description summaries and about me sections for jobseekers on MauKerja Malaysia platforms.

View on Github Research / Applied QA
Problem
LLM outputs are non-deterministic, making them difficult to validate. Generated summaries may contain hallucinations, bias, or missing key information and it's directly affecting job matching quality.

Approach
Built a hybrid evaluation framework combining:
  • Rule-based validation (length, completeness, formatting)
  • Semantic similarity scoring using BERTScore as a weak signal, used to detect major deviations rather than enforce strict correctness
  • Detection of hallucinated or biased terms
  • Human-in-the-loop (HITL) validation for edge cases

System Design
LLM outputs are stored as JSON and processed through an evaluation pipeline for regression detection and quality tracking across model iterations.

Impact
Introduced a repeatable QA process for generative AI systems by enabling regression testing and reducing reliance on manual review.

Key Insight
LLMs require multi-dimensional evaluation, not exact assertions. But quality scoring across constraints, semantics, and human judgment.

Observation
In reality, semantic similarity scores averaged ~0.55 when comparing generated summaries to job descriptions. This somewhat considered as a key limitation: high-quality summaries it's heavily influenced by structure and phrasing. As a result, semantic scoring was treated as a supporting signal, not a source of truth. Thus, reinforcing the need for hybrid evaluation and QA manual review.

Limitations
Golden dataset maintenance, BERTScore blind spots, and HITL scalability remain key challenges for long-term adoption.
Case Study · Advisory / Product
WAIS (Apple Developer Academy Bali)

Served as an independent advisor for a capstone team from Apple Developer Academy Bali (2025 cohort), guiding product, engineering, and QA direction from early-stage chaos to a successful App Store release.

View on AppStore External Advisor
Problem
A 4-person capstone team struggled with misalignment across product, engineering, and research. Challenges included interpersonal conflict, overengineering decisions despite limited resources, unclear research synthesis, and lack of product direction. The team aimed to qualify for the Apple Catalyst Program but lacked a cohesive execution strategy.

Tension
Without clear structure, the team encountered:
  • Frequent conflicts due to unclear roles and expectations
  • Task misalignment leading to inefficiency and frustration
  • Overengineering decisions that slowed progress
  • Confusion in translating user research into actionable product decisions

Decision
After an initial observation phase, I intervened by introducing structure and clarity:
  • Established meaningful daily standups focused on alignment, not formality
  • Enforced clear task prioritization using a structured agile/project board
  • Advised against expanding the team to preserve communication efficiency
  • Worked closely with PMs and engineers to define a North Star metric
  • Guided product decisions through tighter feedback loops and research synthesis
  • Resolved App Store rejection by advising creation of a supporting website for legitimacy compliance

Outcome
The team successfully launched their first production version on the App Store (Feb 2026), gaining ~10 organic users in the first week and completing the full product delivery cycle from ideation to release.

Key Insight
Early-stage teams fail less from technical limitations and more from lack of alignment. Introducing structure, constraints, and shared clarity can unlock execution even with limited resources.
